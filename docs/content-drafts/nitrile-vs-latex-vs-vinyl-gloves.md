# Nitrile vs. Latex vs. Vinyl Gloves: Which Exam Glove Should You Choose?

Choosing an exam glove comes down to four questions: what are you protecting against, does anyone involved have a latex allergy, how much tactile precision does the task demand, and what does your budget per case look like? Here's how the three common materials compare.

## Quick comparison

| | Nitrile | Latex | Vinyl |
|---|---|---|---|
| Material | Synthetic rubber | Natural rubber | PVC |
| Allergy risk | Very low | Latex-allergy risk | Very low |
| Puncture resistance | Highest | High | Lowest |
| Chemical resistance | Excellent | Good | Limited |
| Fit & tactile feel | Excellent (conforms with body heat) | Best-in-class elasticity | Looser fit |
| Typical cost | Mid | Mid–high | Lowest |
| Best for | Clinical care, labs, chemo-rated tasks | High-dexterity procedures where allergy is ruled out | Short, low-risk tasks; food service |

## Nitrile: the clinical default

Nitrile has become the standard exam glove in most clinical settings for three reasons: it eliminates latex-allergy risk for both staff and patients, it offers the best puncture and chemical resistance of the three materials, and modern formulations conform to the hand closely enough that the old "latex feels better" gap has mostly closed.

Choose nitrile when gloves may contact blood, body fluids, or disinfectants; when sharps are involved; or when you simply want one glove that covers most tasks. Many nitrile exam gloves are also tested against chemotherapy drugs — check the box rating if that matters for your facility.

## Latex: unmatched feel, with one big caveat

Natural rubber latex still offers the best elasticity and tactile sensitivity, which is why some surgical and dental teams prefer it for fine work. The caveat is well known: latex proteins can trigger reactions ranging from contact dermatitis to anaphylaxis, and repeated exposure sensitizes staff over time. Many facilities have gone latex-free entirely for that reason.

If you use latex, use powder-free versions (powder carries the allergenic proteins) and keep a nitrile stock for allergic staff and patients.

## Vinyl: the economy option for low-risk tasks

Vinyl gloves cost the least and are fine for brief, low-risk tasks — handling non-hazardous materials, routine cleaning, food prep. They fit more loosely, stretch less, and lose barrier integrity faster under stress, so they are not the right choice where infection control or sharps are in play.

## How to decide

- **Any latex allergy in the room → nitrile** (or vinyl for low-risk tasks).
- **Blood, fluids, sharps, or chemicals → nitrile.**
- **Maximum dexterity, allergy ruled out → latex, powder-free.**
- **Short low-risk tasks at volume → vinyl** keeps cost down.

Also match the size and the box count to your workflow: most exam gloves come 100–300 per box, and sizing runs XS–XL (nitrile and latex conform; vinyl runs loose — consider sizing down).
